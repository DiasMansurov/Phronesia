# Writing Style Guide

Source sample: [RME assignment.docx](/Users/ruslanshaymardanov/Desktop/IAAR Evaluation/RME%20assignment.docx)

This guide captures the recurring voice, tone, logic, and structural habits in the source sample so future writing can sound recognisably like the same author across academic, professional, and more conversational contexts.

## Core Voice

- Analytical and reflective rather than performative.
- Calm, serious, and thoughtful.
- Interested in nuance, context, and limitations.
- Willing to make a claim, but rarely without a qualification or counterpoint.
- Persuasive through reasoning, not through forceful certainty.
- More likely to sound fair-minded than absolute.

## Signature Qualities

- Starts from a broad issue, then narrows toward a practical implication.
- Treats context as essential, not decorative.
- Often identifies a gap in current understanding before proposing a direction.
- Balances one idea against another instead of pushing a single-sided argument.
- Shows intellectual honesty by naming caveats, constraints, and risks.
- Uses first person selectively to explain decisions, intentions, or methodological choices.
- Prefers measured confidence:
  - `it seems`
  - `it appears`
  - `there seems to be`
  - `is likely to`
  - `may`
  - `might`
  - `potentially`
  - `perhaps`

## Logic Pattern

The most common reasoning flow is:

1. State what the field, context, or situation currently looks like.
2. Identify a gap, tension, omission, or inconsistency.
3. Introduce an alternative explanation or added layer of complexity.
4. Weigh caveats or limitations openly.
5. Return to a grounded conclusion or practical next step.

Common author moves:

- `Research has focused on X. However, Y remains underexplored.`
- `This seems persuasive, but it is not without limitations.`
- `One explanation could be X. However, an alternative explanation may be Y.`
- `Therefore, it makes sense to...`
- `Importantly, this is not only a question of X, but also of Y.`

## Sentence and Paragraph Style

- Usually writes in medium-to-long sentences.
- Comfortable with multi-clause reasoning when ideas are layered.
- Paragraphs often build steadily rather than landing one short point at a time.
- Sentences frequently begin with signposting transitions that help the logic feel guided.
- Tends to define the issue, then refine it, then qualify it.

Typical rhythm:

- opening claim
- literature or evidence support
- contrast or caveat
- implication

## Preferred Transitions

These are strong markers of the source voice and should appear naturally in future writing:

- `However`
- `Moreover`
- `Therefore`
- `Similarly`
- `Specifically`
- `Indeed`
- `On the other hand`
- `Firstly`
- `Secondly`
- `Thirdly`
- `Finally`
- `Importantly`
- `Naturally`

They should be used as signposts, not sprinkled mechanically.

## Tone Markers

- Never glib.
- Rarely dramatic.
- Curious rather than defensive.
- Critical without sounding hostile.
- Formal in structure, but still readable.
- Inclined to say `this is important because...` rather than simply assert importance.

## How Claims Are Made

- Claims are usually introduced as provisional but meaningful.
- Strong assertions are often softened by context.
- The writing does not hide uncertainty; it uses uncertainty as part of the reasoning.
- Instead of overstating, it often reframes:
  - not `this proves`
  - more like `this suggests`, `this indicates`, `this appears to support`

## Evidence Habits

- Evidence is woven directly into the line of reasoning.
- In academic mode, citations usually follow the relevant claim immediately.
- Quotations are used sparingly and only when a phrase is especially useful.
- The writing prefers synthesis over quotation.
- Evidence is often followed by interpretation, not left to stand alone.

## Structural Habits

- Uses subheadings clearly.
- Often introduces a section with a framing question.
- Uses enumerated reasoning when complexity rises:
  - `Firstly`
  - `Secondly`
  - `Thirdly`
- Returns repeatedly to the central question so the argument does not drift.
- Ends sections by reconnecting analysis to purpose.

## First-Person Use

First person is used best when:

- explaining a choice
- narrowing a focus
- acknowledging a limitation
- outlining a plan
- clarifying positionality

It should sound deliberate and modest, for example:

- `I plan to...`
- `I will focus on...`
- `This encouraged me to consider...`
- `As a researcher, I am likely to...`

Avoid overly personal or emotive self-reference.

## What Makes the Voice Distinct

The voice is not just "academic." Its distinctive features are:

- It thinks in tensions.
- It respects complexity.
- It makes room for alternative explanations.
- It sounds intellectually responsible.
- It often moves from abstract debate to practical judgment.
- It is comfortable saying that something is promising while still incomplete.

## Adaptation Across Contexts

### Academic

Keep:

- full signposting
- layered reasoning
- explicit caveats
- literature comparison
- cautious claims
- formal structure

Add:

- citations
- conceptual framing
- methodological precision

### Professional

Keep:

- balanced reasoning
- calm authority
- context sensitivity
- clear sequencing
- explicit limitations and implications

Adjust:

- reduce citation density
- replace literature with observations, policy, data, or institutional evidence
- make conclusions more operational

Professional version of the voice should sound:

- thoughtful
- credible
- measured
- strategic

### Friendly

Keep:

- reflective logic
- fairness
- nuanced framing
- willingness to consider more than one side

Adjust:

- shorter sentences
- warmer wording
- less abstract vocabulary
- fewer formal transitions, though some can remain

Friendly version of the voice should sound:

- considerate
- thoughtful
- gently analytical
- clear without sounding stiff

## Style Conversion Rules

When adapting this voice to non-academic writing:

- Preserve the logic, even if the sentence length changes.
- Keep the habit of considering context before judgment.
- Keep the contrastive structure: `yes, but`, `this matters, however`, `one explanation is..., another is...`
- Keep the modest confidence.
- Remove citation mechanics, not the evidence-based mindset.

## Do

- Build arguments step by step.
- Use transitions to guide the reader.
- Name caveats instead of hiding them.
- Compare explanations where useful.
- Tie analysis back to purpose.
- Sound confident, but never overstated.

## Do Not

- Write in a punchy, slogan-heavy style.
- Make flat declarations without support or qualification.
- Use exaggerated certainty.
- Sound salesy, breezy, or performatively clever.
- Collapse complexity too early.
- Overuse emotional language.

## Quick Prompt for Future Use

When writing in this style, aim for:

`Measured, analytical, context-sensitive writing that builds an argument through comparison, caveat, and practical implication. Prefer calm authority over bold certainty. Use clear signposting, recognise limitations openly, and adapt the same reasoning pattern for academic, professional, or friendly contexts.`
